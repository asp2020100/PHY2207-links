The experiment aims to verify the thermodynamic equation of state for a rubber band and measure the constant in this equation at constant temperature. The thermodynamic identity for a rubber band is given by dU=TdS+τdL, where T is the temperature, τ is the tension, U is the internal energy of the rubber band, and S is its entropy[1]. The following discussion outlines the steps involved in the experiment:

1. Measure the tension of a rubber band as a function of temperature and length: Students can use a setup that involves measuring the force as a function of elongation at various fixed temperatures[2]. The elongations will be set by fixed length wires, and so will be the same for the various temperatures. In this way, students can also generate { f(T)L} curves[2].

2. Use Maxwell relations to measure changes in entropy and internal energy: Students can use a Maxwell relation to find the change in internal energy and entropy for an isothermal stretch[4]. This allows students to experimentally check the predictions of the entropic spring model for elastomers and observe that the entropy does indeed decrease as a rubber band is stretched[4].

3. Determine the nonideal contribution to the total force: Students can determine the nonideal contribution to the total force referenced to 30°C for each elongation and tabulate these values[2].

4. Verify Hook's law for a rubber band: Students can verify Hook's law for a rubber band by measuring the force as a function of elongation at various fixed temperatures[3].

5. Measure the constant in the thermodynamic equation of state: Students can measure the constant in the thermodynamic equation of state by using equations (12) and (13) and assuming that the rubber is "ideal" (i.e., that = 0) [2]. Further simplification results from identifying with the "constant-length" heat capacity, CL[2].
